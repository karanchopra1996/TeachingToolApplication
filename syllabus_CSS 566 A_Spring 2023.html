This course covers theories, principles, and practices designed to enable project managers and other related stakeholders to assess, choose and use appropriate frameworks, tools, techniques, and metrics to guide software projects toward successful completion or termination.

Course Overview

This course is about "software management" to manage the systems that produce software-enabled systems. This course covers the science and practice of planning, executing, releasing, and evolving software-enabled systems. It covers theories, principles, and practices designed to enable project/product managers, developers, and other related stakeholders to guide software projects toward successful completion or termination and manage the highly complex software-enabled systems we are building today. This course emphasizes comparing and contrasting different approaches, listening perspectives from Industry Experts in Guest Speaker Series, and making choices based on particular software projects' specific context.

Topics cover theory and practices related to:

  • Project planning, including topics such as ideation, market research, feasibility, requirements gathering, risk management, architectural design, budgeting and staffing, and top-level scheduling
  • Project execution, including topics such as costing, scheduling, specifying, implementing, testing and quality assurance, coordinating processes, synchronization, dependency management, and continuous integration
  • Project release, including topics such as software release life cycles, release planning, triage, driving closure, release stages, and continuous deployment

Learning Outcomes

The defined learning objectives for this course are described here. Each instance of this course will, of necessity, adjust the particular focus across these objectives, but the goal (in general) is to cover them all.

By the end of this course, students should be able to:

  • Use theoretical frameworks to evaluate and assess the applicability of management practices and techniques to different types of software projects.
  • Understand and use a set of tools and techniques for managing a software project.
  • Leverage appropriate quality management theoretical frameworks and practices.
  • Experience the use of management theoretical frameworks, principles, and practices.
  • Create a software project proposal that adheres to underlying theoretical frameworks and best/good practices.
  • Perform formal cost / benefit analysis and budgeting to support organizational objectives, planning, and operations including cost estimation, ROI, etc.
  • Choose and justify a particular set of metrics to use to assess the quality of a software project.

Reference Books:

  • A Guide to the Project Management Body of Knowledge (PMBOK Guide). (2013) (Fifth Edit.). Project Management Institute. This is a definitive treatment of the PMI's view of what a project manager should know. One thing missing from this text is connection to foundational theories that help explain the why that is so important to understand the who, what, when, where, and how.
  • Anderson, D. J. (2004). Agile Management for Software Engineering: Applying the Theory of Constraints for Business Results. Upper Saddle River, NJ: Prentice Hall Professional Technical Reference. A practitioner book connecting to a particular theory: ToC. 
  • Goldratt, E. (2004). The Goal: A Process of Ongoing Improvement (Third Revi.). Great Barrington MA: North River Press. This novel has proved to be an effective way for many people to relate to and begin to more deeply understand the early core of Theory of Constraints. 
  • Appelo, J. (2011). Management 3.0: Leading Agile Developers, Developing Agile Leaders. Addison-Wesley Professional.
  • Berkun, Scott. Making Things Happen: Mastering Project Management. O’Reilly Media, 2008.
  • Humble, Jez, and David Farley. Continuous Delivery. Upper Saddle River, NJ: Addison-Wesley, 2011.
  • McCarthy, Jim. Dynamics of Software Development. Redmond, Washington: Microsoft Press, 1995. One of the early books that focused on the foundational nature of the human side of software development. 

Reference Papers:

  • Brynjolfsson, Erik, and Andrew McAfee. Research Brief. Race Against The Machine: How The Digital Revolution Is Accelerating Innovation, Driving Productivity, and Irreversibly Transforming Employment and The Economy. The Lancet. Diabetes & Endocrinology. Vol. 2, April 2012. The digitization of work is changing our world is fundamental and wide reaching ways. What might this mean?
  • Fitz, Timothy. “Continuous Deployment at IMVU: Doing the Impossible Fifty Times a Day.,” 2009. http://timothyfitz.wordpress.com/2009/02/10/continuous-deployment-at-imvu-doing-the-impossible-fifty-times-a-day/Links to an external site.. "Gone" are the days when delivering a new feature needed to wait for a release train. 
  • Kevin J. Watson, John H. Blackstone, and Stanley C. Gardiner. “The Evolution of a Management Philosophy: The Theory of Constraints.” Journal of Operations Management 25, no. 2 (2007): 387–402. The first part of this paper argues that ToC is foundational to management today. 
  • Larman, Craig, and Victor R Basili. “Iterative and Incremental Development: A Brief History.” Computer 36, no. 6 (2003): 47 – 56. The iterative and incremental nature that is at the core of agile have been around since the beginning of software development. 
  • Snowden, David J, and Mary E Boone. “A Leader’s Framework for Decision Making.” Harvard Business Review 85, no. 11 (2007): 68–76. The Cynefin framework is a powerful and actionable framework for reasoning about the type of work done by many organizations, including software development, and how to manage that work.
  • Sommerville, Ian, Dave Cliff, Radu Calinescu, Justin Keen, Tim Kelly, Marta Kwiatkowska, John Mcdermid, and Richard Paige. “Large-Scale Complex IT Systems: The Reductionism behind Today’s Software-Engineering Methods Breaks down in the Face of Systems Complexity.” Communications of the ACM 55, no. 7 (2012): 71 – 77. Software management deals with complexity. 
  • Tseitlin, Ariel. “The Antifragile Organization.” Communications of the ACM 56, no. 8 (August 01, 2013): 40–44. doi:10.1145/2492007.2492022. A fascinating account of how Netflix attacks its live systems to ensure a more resilient system.

Protocols Regarding Internet Issues

Because the class will have lessons taught remotely, we are all dependent on the Internet.  Here is the protocol we will follow to handle Internet issues:

  • Instructor Internet issues during class:  If my (the instructor's) Internet goes out in class, please remain connected for 10 minutes.  If I can get things up and running during that time, I'll continue class as normal.  If after 10 minutes I have not returned, assume that class is cancelled and that I will post a recorded lecture on Canvas for the remainder of the class at my earliest convenience.  I will try to message the class from my smart phone if my mobile Internet will be working.
  • Student Internet issues during class:  If you lose Internet access:
    • During a regular class, try to join us as soon as you can.  If there are activities you missed that you want to discuss with me, please do so at your earliest convenience.
    • While submitting a non-exam assignment, please let me know and I'll unlock the assignment to enable you to submit it.  When you do submit it, please include a screenshot of your file's Properties window (Windows) or Get Info window (Mac) that shows that the modification date of the file is before the assignment's due date.
    • While submitting an exam/quiz, please let me know and I will work with your specific situation.  This may involve screenshots showing the file's modification date (as described earlier for non-exam assignments) or other accommodations.  If your Internet is out for an extended period (e.g., days), I may give you a make-up oral exam over Zoom. 

If you are dropped from a Canvas exam/quiz while you are taking it, when you return, Canvas will put you where you were left off.
